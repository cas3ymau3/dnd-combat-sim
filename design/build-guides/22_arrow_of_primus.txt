CONCEPT NOTES: 
Mechanically, the basic concept of this build is to be an archer who makes devastating CHA-based attacks with a longbow. To make this viable, we go with devotion paladin for their sacred weapon channel divinity ability, which let's them use CHA for attacks with a weapon they're holding. We then go great-weapon master, which in the updated 2024 PHB applies to attacks made with a longbow. Then we go sorcerer to get quicken spell. When it's all up and running, we'll be able to make 2 CHA-based longbow attacks that benefit from GWM each turn, then quicken true-strike to make a 3rd with our bonus action. We then stack weapon attack buffs from divine favor, magic weapon, hunter's mark, and agonizing blast to boost our damage even further. We also get some really nice capstone abilities at level-19 and 20 that put this build over the top. The nice thing about the way we've set this up, is that we actually make use of the +1 to STR that we have to take with GWM to facilitate our multiclass into paladin. This let's us maintain decent ability scores in other key stats while still going fully into CHA.  

Thematically, I think we want to think of this character as a kind of `steampunk knight' who fights with a magically enhanced bow. Something in our backstory connects us to the foundations of modern civilization, and we have a great admiration for their stories and accomplishments. Perhaps an ancestor was one of the founders of the city where we live, or was a great architect of the city's grand palaces and structures, or a great scholar or intellectual. Whatever the case, this personal connection has led us to be an amateur student of history, economics, and magical technology. Prior to adventuring, we worked in the family business as a merchant, buying and selling alchemical supplies, enchantment materials, and minor magic item across the world/region. The way that trade and economic systems integrate and enrich people's lives has always appealed to us. But we find that in our day-to-day work, we are bombarded by chaos and irrationality. This distresses us, perhaps to the point of depression and anxiety. We find solace in the quiet pursuit of our craft -- in the balancing of the account books, or in the orderly loading and stocking of wagons, boats, and caravans. We love to watch the merchant ships dock and unload their goods in the early morning, before any of the crowds are out, or the master engineers equilibrate the brumestone engines on newly constructed giant airships, or the master enchanters transform as they transform inert materials, runes, and incantations into realized magical objects. We are fascinated by the politics of our land, and have respect/admiration for leaders we consider enlightened and reasonable, and scorn/distaste for those who seem impetuous, fickle, fanatical, or ignorant. 

As our character grows and progresses, we first identify that we derive peace and insight from the logical arrangement of structure. That this has always been a part of us. Eventually, we come to believe that our personal experience is a microcosm for the larger world, and we commit ourselves to the veneration and protection of order. We commit ourselves to the idea that a logical, sensible, and just society is the prerequisite for human(oid) flourishing and that only through the technocratic advancement of civilization can we achieve a better world. Perhaps as we move towards the end of our career, we will soften our dogmatism a bit, and come to recognize that there is a balance between order and chaos. That one only exists in opposition to the other,  and thus form the alpha and omega of the cosmos. That creativity and innovation can arise from idiosyncrasy and that the most fertile ground for human ingenuity involves both order and chaos. Structure and unpredictability. Logic and feeling. 

Building off this theme, we will RP this character as someone who's charisma emerges from their intelligence/clear-thinking, and worldliness/sophistication. We are personable because of our insightful self-awareness, and our genuine curiosity/inquisitiveness. We are likeable because of our honesty and fairness. We are a persuasive debater, and capable of convincing deceptions because of our ability to accurately infer what other people think. 

The manifestation of order in our physical body will be raised lines and dots under our skin that look like traces on a circuit board. At the beginning of our career these traces will be scarce and faint, perhaps only on the back of one hand, or a simple set of traces above our one eye. When we cast spells, I imagine light traveling along these lines, like electricity. For example, when we cast true-strike, I imagine there will be a flash in our eyes, followed by a pulse of electricity along the circuit traces originating from our eye and going to our hand where we hold the bow. As we progress in our career, I imagine we will become more `cyborg-like'. For example, the circuit traces on our body could become more prevalent, and more complex, perhaps creating a beautiful pattern across the entirety of our body. A lot of our spellcasting will also have this kind of `cyborg' vibe (ex.,  hunter's mark as a luminous crosshair that appears in our eye and on the target).

NOTE: Ok so here we go with level-1. Let's start off with a few comments on character creation. We go with human for our species in order to get an additional skill proficiency and an additional origin feat. For our background, we create a custom background that we're calling 'magic supplies merchant'. This is almost identical to the merchant background in the new PHB, except we've switched out animal handling for arcana in our skill proficiencies, and switched out navigator's tools for alchemist's supplies in our tool proficiencies. This is just to give our character a bit more arcane knowledge and intellectual flavor. It will have very limited impact on our game play so I think it's fair. In terms of our origin feats, we go with alert for our extra human feat, and we get lucky from the merchant background. Getting the alert feat is one of the main reasons for going human with this character. We will have low DEX for our entire career, so we really like that the alert feat lets us add our PB to our initiative rolls. As an archer/ranged attacker, we want to be able to position ourselves advantageously at the start of combat, and this is going to help us do that. Alert is also a great for overall party tactics, as the initiative swap ability can help your team set up powerful synergies and combos. Thematically, our character is going to be all about order and will be using our inherent magic to bend the world around us towards a desirable structure/organization. The alert feat literally lets us manipulate the order of combat, so it's very on brand for us. Lucky is the same. We can think of luck points as us exerting our magical will to nudge reality towards a desired set of outcomes, a desired structure. I'm imagining our character shooting an arrow that seems like it's going to miss, but its trajectory suddenly changes at the last second (when we use a luck point) in order to turn the miss into a hit. Luck points are also similar mechanically to the `restore balance' feature that we will get from clockwork sorcerer, and the epic boon that we will get all the way at the end of our career at level-20. So, overall, on theme. Luck is also great for us since, for much of our career, we're going to be landing a relatively small number of `big hits' on our turn. So more than other characters, I think we will like the ability to give ourselves advantage on those. It also dovetails nicely with our `innate sorcery' feature to help with landing saving throw spells. Innate sorcery let's us bump our spell save DC by 1. Then we can also make a target roll the save with disadvantage using Luck. 

In terms of skill proficiencies, from sorcerer I took insight and deception. From our background, I took arcana and persuasion. And for our extra human skill proficiency, I took history. Our background as a purveyor of magical materials gives us proficiency in arcana. Our connection to and interest in the great civilizations of old is reflected in our proficiency in history. Insight and persuasion are social skills that we developed while being a merchant. And deception comes from our cleverness. We have a good sense of how people think / how they will react. So, when it suits us, we use can effectively `press people's buttons' to manipulate them. See the concept note above for a longer explanation. The alchemist's supplies tool proficiency is on-brand for our background, and it also provides the mechanical advantage of allowing us to craft potions (of healing) in our downtime if we so choose. This also feels like something we should be able to do given our background. Ok, enough on skills. 

For equipment, we go with the gold-buy method and pick up alchemist's supplies (expensive!) a light-crossbow, some crossbow bolts, an arcane focus (I think rod is most appropriate) and an explorer's pack. The light crossbow is going to be our best combat option for the first couple of levels here. It's on a d8 damage die, which is good for a simple weapon. But it has the loading and two-handed property. So we can only make one attack with it on our turn and we have to have two free hands. For now, neither of those things is a problem for us, so we'll take the extra damage. Eventually we'll switch this out once we start wanting to make multiple attacks on our turn. Alright, and we don't have any armor proficiencies, so we don't wear any armor which puts our AC at a measly 11. Barf, but we're going to be staying as far away from enemies as we can for now. 

Alright, so then for class features, as a sorcerer we of course get spellcasting. For cantrips, we take true-strike, sorcerous burst, shocking grasp, and light. True-strike is going to be our go to, allowing us to make attacks using our crossbow with CHA instead of DEX. For reference, here is the updated text for true-strike:

Sorcerous burst is a new sorcerer-exclusive cantrip that does 1d8 damage (at level-1) with the potential to do additional damage if you roll an 8. On average, it's still less damage than true-striking with our crossbow, since we get to add our CHA modifier to true-strike attacks. But sorcerous burst does let you choose the damage type (from options: acid, fire, cold, lightning, thunder, psychic, poison) when you cast it, so it's good to have in our back pocket for overcoming resistances. Plus, it will benefit from cantrip scaling as we level up so it'll always be a ranged damage option. This could be useful if we find ourselves in situations where we don't have our weapons, or can't consistently make CHA based weapon attacks. We take shocking grasp in case we get stuck in melee and need to disengage. It's also on-theme for our mechanical/cyborg themed spellcasting. Notably, however, it was nerfed in 2024. Now it only prevents targets hit by it from making opportunity attacks, rather than from taking any kind of reaction whatsoever. Still good enough for us. Finally we take the light cantrip since we are a lowly human who lacks darkvision. We will also be a pretty light/radiant themed character, so it's on brand. 

In terms of leveled-spells, we get 2 spells -- and I think we take mage armor and the jump spell. Mage armor and shield are the two premium options to deal with our pathetic AC. In my opinion, mage armor is slightly better than shield for us right now. We only have 2 spell slots, so we can't cast shield a lot. And mage armor gives us a base AC of 14 for a full 8 hours. But whatever, if you prefer shield then go with that. As for the jump spell, the updated version of it is super good -- providing the option to jump 30 ft for 10ft of movement on your turn for the duration of the spell. It will be a staple mobility skill for our character. Plus it upcasts well and works with twinned spell (though we may not take advantage of that till much later). As an archer, I imagine we will always be trying to find the `high ground' where we can see our enemies and they can't reach us. Being able to jump 30ft on our turn will be very helpful in attaining it. It's also a bonus action to cast, and we don't have anything good to do with our BA right now, except for innate sorcery. Plus, I think it fits easily into our kind of mechanical/cyborg magical aesthetic. Like, I could easily imagine a pulse of magical energy running through the circuit-board on our body and producing pulse of arcane energy that propels us upward for a big jump. You know, kind of like `engage thrusters'.  

In addition to spellcasting, we get the new (to the 2024 PHB) sorcery innate sorcery base class feature. This ability is a bonus action to activate, we can do it twice per long rest, and when we activate it for the next 1m we get +1 to our spell save DC and we get advantage on attack rolls of sorcerer spells we cast. This is an awesome limited use combat steroid. Right now at level-1, we don't have any spells that force saving throws. But innate sorcery still gives us automatic advantage on attacks we make with true-strike, which is our bread-and-butter attack cantrip. So we love it. I think we can also imagine this feature as operating in the same thematic way as our luck points and heroic inspiration. A kind of magical altering of the grand equation of the cosmos to change outcomes in real time. So that's great, too. 

In terms of combat tactics -- try to precast mage armor if you can. Seize the initiative with the alert feat (we have a +3 to initiative rolls right now), use jump to get to an advantageous spot, then make a true-strike attack with the light crossbow as our action to do 1d8 + 3 = 7.5 damage. Thematically, I would imagine our crossbow as more like a `pulse rifle' and would definitely use the option in true-strike to change the weapon damage type to radiant. Use innate sorcery, luck points, or the heroic advantage we get each day as a human to make the attacks stick. Alright, that's it for level-1. It's always a long one. But we did it, so let's move on.

NOTE: Ok we're level-2 and we're going sorcerer-2. First off, we get the font of magic base class feature, which endows us with 2 sorcery points, and gives us the ability to convert spell slots into sorcery points and vice versa. In addition, we get to pick two metamagic options that we can use our sorcery points to fuel. The main one we need for this build is quickened spell, which let's us cast a spell that normally has a casting time of 1 action as a bonus action, subject to the constraint that you can't use this ability to cast two spells of level-1 or higher on the same turn. Note that language of quicken spell prohibits casting two-leveled spells on the same turn, regardless of whether or not they consume spell slots. So even though we now have the `only one spell slot per turn' rule in the 2024 PHB, we still can't use quickened spell to cast a level-1 or higher spell as a BA and another level-1 or higher spell as an action, even if one of those is cast without a spell slot. This won't matter for us much, but it's worth clarifying. 

The main thing we're going to be doing with quickened spell is using it to cast true-strike with our Bonus Action. Now in order to actually do this, we have to switch out our primary weapon over to the shortbow. This is because the light crossbow has the loading property, which means you can only make 1 attack per turn. The shortbow does a bit less damage, but that's just the cost of doing business. So now we can make one true-strike attack on our turn with our shortbow as an action, then use quickened spell to make an additional true-strike attack using our bonus action. When we do this, we will do a very respectable 2d6 + 6 = 13 damage on our turn. Pretty good for level-2. Now, quickened spell costs 2 sorcery points (SP), and right now we only have 2 SP. We can also convert our spell slots into SP at a ratio of 1 SP per spell slot level. But even if we convert 2 level-1 spell slots to sorcery point sin order to give ourselves 4 SP, we can only do our BA true strike attack a maximum of twice per long rest. Not great, but not terrible either. 

For our second metamagic option -- I'm going to recommend taking extended spell. When you use extended spell when casting a spell with a duration of 1m or longer, you double the spell's duration (up to 24h) and, if the spell requires concentration, you have advantage on saving throws to maintain concentration. This latter feature is new in the 2024 PHB. We like extended spell for a few reasons. First, our character is going to be using a number of spells that passive buffs for a period of time (e.g., mage armor, divine favor, magic weapon, etc.). These are much easier to precast if you can double their duration. Now this won't always be worth the sorcery points, but sometimes it very much will be (especially at later levels). For example, right at level-2 we can easily use extended spell when we cast mage armor to increase the duration to 16 hours, pretty much guaranteeing that we will have it up for the entire day. Second, as our character progresses, we are going to be relying on a number of key concentration spells to enhance our character's mobility and utility (e.g., spider climb, fly, circle of power, etc.). Since we'll be converting a lot of our spell slots into sorcery points to fuel bonus action attacks, it's going to be critical that we be able to maintain concentration on these spells when we cast them. Extended spell will help with this, in addition to boosting their duration. 

Ok, finally, in addition to sorcery points and metamagic, at sorcerer-2 we also get to another 1st-level spell slot, and we get to prepare two new sorcerer spells. Considering my recommendation is to burn *two* of our 1st-level slots to create 2 sorcery points, this means we will only have 1 level-1 slot left with which to cast spells. As a default, if there's an chance we'll end up in combat, then we probably need to cast mage armor. This leaves us with *no* spell slots for any new spells. Since we're currently in this situation, what we want are spells that we can cast as rituals. Comprehend languages and detect magic both fit the bill. Both are also, in my opinion, very thematic. I imagine our character's spellcasting as having a kind of mechanical/cyborg-like quality -- with magical effects manifesting as arcane technological enhancements. And I can certainly imagine our character sending a magic pulse to her ears enabling her to understand any spoken language, or activating a lens of magic runes in her eye that allows her to read any writing. And `activating' detect magic as a kind of sensory scanner also seems consistent with this kind of aesthetic. Both also work well with our extended spell metamagic, if we happen to still have a sorcery point left to use. 

Ok, well that's it for level-2. Moving on!

NOTE: Ok we're level-3 and we're going sorcerer-3. We get 3 new spell slots (two lvl-2 and another lvl-1), 2 new prepared spells, and, of course, we get our sorcerer subclass. For our subclass, if it wasn't obvious, we're going with clockwork soul. The affinity for civilization, structure, law and order defines this character and officially, at this level, becomes the source of their magical power. In terms of narrative arc, I would say that in taking this subclass, our character has developed a self awareness of this fact, and become cognizant of the fact that they draw power from the sublime order and beautiful, harmonic organization of the cosmos. They have recognized that they find peace and strength in those aspects of their life that embody these concepts, and begin to more strongly gravitate towards those activities.

Mechanically, when we take this subclass, we get a few things. First we get the `restore balance' feature, which allows us to use our reaction to neutralize advantage or disadvantage on a d20 roll being made by a creature we can see within 60 ft of us. The only update to this feature in the 2024 PHB is that now we can use it a number of times per day equal to our CHA modifier (so, for us, 3 times). Previously the number of uses was based on your proficiency bonus. This is a great ability. It provides great flavor for the subclass theme, and dovetails nicely with our lucky origin feat. It is also a great way to provide utility in combat that doesn't require any of our -- at this point -- very scarce spellcasting resources. So we definitely love that. 

Next, we get the `clockwork magic' feature. This feature provides us with bound spells when we reach certain sorcerer levels. Here at level-3 we get alarm and protection from evil and good. We always have these spells prepared, but they don't count against the number of spells we can prepare. Neither is great. Alarm is a ritual so we like that. And protection against evil and good is a strong defensive option in the right situations. Unfortunately, in the 2024 PHB this feature was also significantly nerfed, and we can no longer swap out these clockwork spells for other spells on the warlock/wizard lists when we gain levels. This is a huge bummer, as it significantly reduces the flexibility of spell selection for this subclass. And if I were DM'ing, I would 100% let players swap out spells as per the old clockwork magic feature. But this nerf is offset by expanded base class spell list for sorcerers in the 2024 PHB, as well as by the other base class features that sorcerers gained. For this guide, I'm going to try to stick to the RAW, and that means keeping the clockwork spells we are given. 

Ok so what new spells do we choose to prepare? Well, to start we're actually going to take two transmutation spells: magic weapon and spider climb. We like both of these spells a lot. Magic weapon has been updated in the 2024 PHB, and no longer requires concentration. So we can cast it on our shortbow (possibly enhanced with extended spell) and give it a +1 to attack and damage rolls. We get especially good mileage out of this when we use our action and bonus action to make crossbow true-strike attacks. With magic weapon up, our special double true-strike attack would do 2d6 + 8  = 15 damage. Pretty good. As for spider climb, it is a premier early-level mobility/defensive option for any character that makes ranged attacks. Walk up on to the ceiling and rain down crossbow bolts on melee enemies who have no way to reach you. And while it does require concentration, we do have proficiency in CON saves and we can use extended spell to give ourselves advantage on concentration checks if we have to. Plus we have lucky and our human heroic advantage. So we should feel very confident using spider climb and going wherever we please. And lastly, we're going to drop comprehend languages and take the new `arcane vigor' spell available to sorcerers. The details of this spell are below, but the tldr is that it's a bonus action heal with hit dice that scales off your spellcasting ability modifier (instead of CON) and upcasts well. Now, this is a case where I'm prioritizing the mechanical advantages of the feat over the fit with the character. Our character has no connection to the feywild. We're not an elf. Hunter's Mark
Level-1 Divination (Ranger) 
 - casting time: bonus action 
 - range: 90ft 
 - components: V 
 - duration: concentration, 1hr
 - description: You choose one creature you can see within range and magically mark it as your quarry.

Ok so it's still a bonus action to cast. It still requires concentration. It still gives you advantage on perception checks to find the marked target, and it still adds 1d6 damage when you hit a marked enemy with a weapon attack. The key differences are that (1) the extra damage from hunter's mark is now Force damage, rather than the same damage type your weapon (this is great for overcoming damage resistances), and (2) that the 2024 version of the spell can be upcast. Now when you cast hunter's mark with a spell slot of 3 or 4 the damage increases to 2d6 each time we hit, and the duration goes up to 8h. And if we use a 5th level slot or higher, the damage goes up to 3d6 and the duration increases to 24h. The increased damage scaling is the main reason we want hunter's mark, and the main reason we poohpooh-ed the hex spell when we took our warlock dip. So having spells that upcast well is going to be key. Hunter's mark is great for this, and will officially our best concentration option for damage moving forward. As an additional aside, it's worth highlighting that hunter's mark at lvl-3 or lvl-4 also works very well with extended spell, as it would then last for a full 16h (if we can maintain concentration), potentially allowing us to help track an elusive quarry over an entire adventuring day.

And if we need to dish out max damage, we can convert another lvl-2 slot into sorcery points to give ourselves another quickened spell bonus action attack (up to 5 per day). 

To conclude, we do a quick damage check. And things have improved for us a lot. We got a boost to our CHA modifier, we got cantrip scaling and extra damage from agonizing blast. In general, it's really nice to see this character starting to come together. Ok, huge essay here at level-5. Let's move on.

Through this connection, and through our own journey of self-discovery we have come to understand that we ourselves draw strength from the grand order, structure, and organization of the cosmos -- calculations of the grand equation that spin out reality. And as a result of this realization, we have begun allocating our time towards those activities (such as they are) in adventuring that embody these notions of orderliness, structure, and control. Now, as we move into taking paladin levels, I think our character's journey will embark in a slightly new direction. Whereas much of our previous development has been internally focused, now we begin to question how we should exert ourselves upon the external world around us. In thinking through this question, our character will come to realize that they want to dedicate themselves to the principles of order, and promote peace, and human flourishing by making manifest structure, law, efficiency, and fairness in the world around them. This will eventually lead to our taking an oath of devotion to these principles. And that's how I would give narrative shape to this character's particular multiclass combination. Not too difficult, actually. 

Ok narrative stuff aside, going paladin-1 gets us a couple key things. First off, we get medium armor mastery. So let's (finally!) put on some decent medium armor (breastplate) to bring our AC up to 15. Still not good, but  we also no longer need to cast mage armor just to avoid certain death. Hooray! We also get martial weapon proficiency and weapon mastery. So we can switch out our shortbow and pick up a longbow. Switching to the longbow is actually a boost to our offensive and defensive capabilities. The longbow has a range of 150ft (versus 80ft for the shortbow), so we can stay even further away from enemies, reducing our damage intake. It also has the slow mastery property, meaning we can slow down enemies trying to come and get us. There's also another way we can go with this, though I would only use it situationally. Taking paladin levels also gets us shield proficiency. And we do get a second weapon mastery (we use one on longbows). So one thing we could do is start carrying a shield and make thrown weapon attacks with javelins using true-strike. This will do less damage than the longbow, and require that we be much closer to our enemies (javelins have a range of 30ft). But we would be able to up our armor class to AC-17. Personally, I think of this character as an archer, so I'm going to go with the longbow. But, hey, if the situation calls for it, why not make use of those extra proficiencies/weapon masteries. 

Paladin-1 also gets us 5 point of lay on hands bonus action healing, which is a nice supplement to what we already have with arcane vigor. In terms of paladin spells, there's one big one that we really care about, and that's divine favor. Updated for the 2024 PHB, divine favor is now a bonus action to cast, lasts a minute, and *doesn't* require concentration (!). While the spell is active all your weapon attacks do an addition 1d4 radiant damage on hit. Ok so the big change is that it's no longer concentration. This means we can stack it with hunter's mark and magic weapon to make our longbow true-strike attacks deal some pretty nasty damage. More precisely, if we have an upcast lvl-3 hunter's mark on an enemy, and we've managed to cast both magic weapon and divine favor on ourselves, then our basic double true-strike combo attack (with agonizing blast) would deal: 

2d8 (longbow) + 4d6 (hunter's mark) + 2d6 (true-strike) + 2d4 (divine favor) + 16 (4*CHA) + 2 (magic weapon) = 
53 damage.

Not too shabby! Now, obviously it takes a bit of work/time to get all our buffs in place (divine favor and hunter's mark are both bonus actions, for example). But this is pretty great damage for this level, and we're going to take it even further. So yeah, thanks divine favor! 

For our second paladin spell, I would say, pick your favorites. For me, I chose cure wounds. So let's start with the big stuff. Extra-attack officially changes the game for us. Now our best damage attack combo is no longer the `double true-strike combo'. So even though we would use true-strike if we wanted to make the strongest single attack we possibly could, doing more attacks produces more damage on our turn. Here's a brief damage comparison: 

Assume we have precast magic weapon (at lvl-4), upcast hunter's mark (at lvl-3), cast divine favor, and activated sacred weapon. Then the new premier damage combo, a.k.a. `attack-attack-true-strike' would look like: 

attack(1):longbow(GWM) + attack(2):longbow(GWM) + BA:true-strike(longbow)_{quickened spell, agonizing blast}

And this would do: 

3d8(longbow) + 6d6(hunter's mark) + 2d6(true-strike) + 3d4(divine favor) + 16(4*CHA) + 
8(GWM) + 6(magic-weapon) =  79 damage

By contrast, the standard double true-strike combo would only do: 

2d8(longbow) + 4d6(hunter's mark) + 4d6(true-strike) + 2d4(divine-favor) + 16(4*CHA) + 
4(magic-weapon) = 62 damage

So the new approach increases our max damage combo by 27%, on average, which is a big jump. 

Now let's talk about the steed. So new to the 2024 PHB, all paladin's get the `faithful steed' feature at level 5, which lets them learn the find steed spell, always have it prepared, and cast it for free (at lvl-2) once per day. Mechanically, this is actually going to be quite good for us. Up to this point (or at least for the past several levels), the premier mobility/defensive option that we have been using in combat is the fly spell. Well, when upcast at 4th level, the otherworldly steed summoned by find steed has a fly speed of 60ft. Additionally, as a controlled mount that acts on our initiative in combat, we can use it's movement and command it to take the Dash action on our turn -- ostensibly giving us 120ft of flying movement per turn. It also lasts indefinitely, or rather it only disappears if it is killed, we dismiss it, or we die. For comparison the fly spell only gives us flight for 10m. But that's not all. The steed can also make an attack (using our spell modifier for the attack and damage roll) and gains a special bonus action determined by the type of steed we summon (fey, fiend, or celestial). More on that in a second. Finally, and perhaps most importantly, it doesn't require our concentration, freeing us up to cast hunter's mark while flying. The general point is that, at a baseline, find steed gives us more mobility and more action economy than our previous best mobility option. So we really like this spell. Now, to give it a fly speed, we need to use a 4th level spell slot. And we only have two of those. But, if we're careful, we can do this once and have the steed last for multiple adventuring days. Alternatively, if we want a budget option, we can use our free casting to summon the steed at level-2, and then cast jump on it to give it additional (vertical) mobility. Which of these will be the best option depends on the context, but I would probably shell out for the level-4 steed and see if I could make it last, and then use the budget steed (or revert back to casting fly) in a pinch. 

Right, so now let's talk about the special bonus actions. When we summon the steed we can choose whether we want it to be a fey, celestial, or fiend. Each different type gets a special bonus action ability that we can use once per long rest. If we choose fiend, we get the 'fell glare' ability which can frighten a target. If we choose the celestial steed, then we get the `healing touch' ability which restores some hit points. And if we choose the fey steed we get the `fey step' ability which let's us (steed and rider) teleport up to 60ft. For me, I think the fey option is the clear winner, with the celestial coming in second. A free 60ft teleport once per day, on top of the additional mobility the mount provides? Yes, please! I think this is more valuable than a pretty decent heal, as we can use the extra mobility to stay out of danger, or even to rush over and heal our allies if we need to. Plus, we have the thematic consistency with the free misty-step our character gets from the fey-touched feat. So yeah, I think we should be summoning the fey steed. 

Now, let's talk about the thematics. I don't actually love them. First off, I kind of agree with some of the recent critiques of the `faithful steed' feature that have arisen since it was confirmed to be in the 2024 PHB. Like, not all paladin's have a mount. It's a class that can be played in a wide variety of ways, and it seems awkward to give every single one this mount-based ability that you either shoe-horn into your RP or feel bad about not using. For our character, I don't think it's sooo bad. I mean, I don't imagine them with a mount as a baseline, but I think it can be reflavored to make sense. And we really like the mechanical advantages the feature provides. So I'm happy to reflavor it as needed. Right so speaking of flavor -- while we want the fey themed mount, I don't actually think this is very thematically consistent with our character. In fact, none of the options are, really. I mean, I suppose we could choose the celestial option, but it doesn't seem like a great fit. I mean, if anything our character's identity is quite strongly based out of the world of the material plane, with our powers having a kind of techno-mechanical vibe. Doesn't scream angel horse to me. If anything, we have somewhat of a proclivity for abjuring extra-planar beings and sending them back to their own planes of existence. So it feels weird to summon one. Finally, as we discussed when we took the fey-touched feat, the fey realm is kind of the least compatible with our character's neutral, rational, and logical way of viewing the world. But here we are and we want to summon a fey horse. So my recommendation is to reflavor the steed as some kind of mechanical steed from Mechanus. It will still technically be fey on paper, but it will RP like a kind of animated machine that we can summon and ride. I'm imagining a star-wars hover-speeder type thing, or maybe a clockwork horse kind of deal. But get creative with it. And then, we reflavor the mount's fey-step ability in the same way we did our own misty-step ability from fey-touched -- i.e. as more of a `warp-step' teleport, rather than a puff of fey mist. 

Wow, ok so we've talked about extra attack and we've talked about faithful steed. Now let's talk about spellcasting. Perhaps the simplest place to start is the bound spells. At level-5, the `paladin's smite' feature lets us learn the shining smite spell, which we won't have much use for unless we decide to go into melee. Shining smite imposes blindness (in addition to damage), which is a decent control effect. So there may be situations where we might want to use it. But probably not. As a level-5 oath of devotion paladin we also get two new bound spells. First off, we learn aid, which we already knew -- more redundancy, bummer. But we also get zone of truth. Zone of truth is not a spell that I usually go for, but I actually kind of love it on this character. It fits really nicely into our `bending reality to the order/structure we desire' theme of our magical abilities and I can imagine some ways to flavor the way the spell looks that fit nicely with our technomagic vibe. It also provides us with great out-of-combat social-interaction utility. In combination with our high CHA, charm spells, and proficiencies in insight, persuasion, and deception -- zone of truth will make us an excellent interrogator. And it's nice to add to this dimension of the character without having to sacrifice our limited set of prepared spells to do so. 

In terms of prepared spells, at paladin-5 we get to prepare 6 paladin spells, which is only one more than paladin-4. But we can now prepare level-2 paladin spells. Usually, this means we get access to some juicy new options, but between our sorcerer spells and our bound spells, we actually already know/have prepared almost all the spells that we would actually want from the lvl-2 paladin spell list (aid, find steed, lesser restoration, magic weapon, zone of truth). The most appealing options remaining (in my opinion) are prayer of healing, warding bond, and locate object. We could also take protection from poison, to lean into our `restore balance' theme, but it's very situational, so I wouldn't stock it as a default. Now, usually I love warding bond, but it's not a great fit for this character since we are not a super tanky character and we will be trying to concentrate on some important spells (ex. hunter's mark). So that leaves prayer of healing and locate object. I think prayer of healing should be an auto-include. It's a good out-of-combat AOE heal for up to six creatures, and benefits from our high spellcasting ability modifier. I also imagine casting it as a kind of `restorative field' which is thematically consistent with a number of our other abilities. As for locate object -- it's kind of one of those spells that you don't know you need until you need it. So it's nice to have an excuse to stock it here. And it's probably more useful than any of the other lvl-1 or lvl-2 spells we could take instead. It also has some fun RP potential as an extension of our `sensing/detection' magic theme. So cool enjoy that. 

I think we also take the opportunity to make some other small adjustments to our spell list. In particular, now that we are going to be using our channel divinity for exclusively for sacred weapon, we won't be using it for divine sense. Because of that, I opted to switch out the command spell for detect evil and good. The latter is just a bit more thematically consistent with our magic identity (though command isn't bad), and I think there's stiffer competition for our actions in combat. So it's less likely that we'll want to cast command than it is for some out-of-combat situation to arise where detect evil and good can be handy. I suppose if you prefer command to heroism, I could see doing that. But I like having heroism on hand as a way to provide immunity to fear. 

Ok so that's all on the spell choices. Now let's finish up by discussing resource management. The first thing that is worth noting is that we are now pretty reliant on sacred weapon to do our best damage in combat. We start with two uses of this ability, get 1 back on a short rest, and can also recover one by casting catnap. This gives us 4 uses per day, which should be enough for most days. Plus, if we're ever stuck in combat without sacred weapon, we can just fall back on the `double true-strike' combo and we'll be fine. But yeah, also thank god for catnap. Now, thinking about spell slots and sorcery points gets a little more complicated here because of what we're doing with find steed. I think it's a bit too conservative to assume that we're casting find steed at lvl-4 *every day*. I mean, hopefully we can keep our steed alive at least some of the time. Especially since we are literally using it so that we can stay out of the fray and fly safely overhead while shooting arrows. But it's not clear how often we'll actually need to cast it. So I think what I'll do is lay out the resource management regime I would use assuming we have already have our steed. Then, if we need to summon the steed, we would just keep (rather than convert) our second 4-lvl slot and use it to cast the spell. So this would reduce our effective sorcery point pool by 4, meaning two fewer bonus action true-strikes. Alright, but assuming we do have our steed with us, here's what I would recommend in terms of resource management on a standard adventuring day: 

 - lvl-1
   * start with 5_{1} lvl-1 slots
   * convert 1 pact magic and 1 sorcerer slots to sorcery points = +2 sorcery points 
   * now we have 3 slots left
   * recover 1 pact magic slot from catnap and convert it to a sorcery point = +1 sorcery point 
   * recover 1 pact magic slot from a short rest and convert it to a sorcery point = +1 sorcery point
   * TOTAL = +4 sorcery points and 3 lvl-1 spell slot for casting during the day 
 -lvl-2 
   * start with 3 lvl-2 slots 
   * convert 1 lvl-2 slot into sorcery points = +2 sorcery points 
   * TOTAL = +2 sorcery points and 2 lvl-2 slots for casting during the day
 - lvl-3 
   * start with 3 lvl-3 slots
   * use 1 lvl-3 slot on catnap 
   * now we have 2 slots left 
   * convert 1 lvl-3 slot into sorcery points = +3 sorcery points 
   * TOTAL = +3 sorcery points and 1 lvl-3 slot for casting during the day 
 - lvl-4 
   * start with 2 lvl-4 slots 
   * convert 1 lvl-4 slot into sorcery points = +4 sorcery points 
 - sorcerous restoration = +2 sorcery points 
 EFFECTIVE SORCERY POINTS: 5 + 4 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 2 = 20 = 10 BA true-strike attacks 
 EFFECTIVE SPELL SLOTS: 3-2-1-1

With this distribution, we would probably be able to make BA true-strike attacks as often as we want. I mean, with 10 BA true-strikes in the bank, we could do 3 per combat for each of 3 combats per day. Given that many combats don't even last three rounds, I imagine that that's probably enough. And, with our remaining spell slots we can still: upcast magic weapon, upcast hunter's mark, cast divine favor a number of times, or cast any of our other spells at each spell level, as needed. Right, so I think at this point we've probably reached saturation with the number of BA true-strike attacks we can do per day and we won't need to convert any more spell slots into sorcery point fodder. So moving forward, we can think about additional spell slots we gain as just adding to the resources we have available to cast spells, which is great.
