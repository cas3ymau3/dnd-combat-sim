MECHANICAL CONCEPT NOTES:
 - magic stealer rouge drain magic and empowered sneak attack features can be used on spells you cast on yourself (ex., shield of faith, detect magic)
 - this provides a means for a class/subclass that is starved for spell slots (e.g., profane soul blood hunter) to gain deal more damage and cast more spells 
 - even with just 1 pact magic slot, with drain magic we can cast a concentration spell in each of 4 combats (recharge 1 on SR and x2 w/drain magic) 
 - so we take magic initiate cleric at character creation to get access to shield of faith --> this provides a target for empowered sneak attack in combat
   and a target for drain magic once combat is over; using shield of faith offsets the defensive loss from dual wielding while empowered sneak attack 
   provides us with additional damage scaling
 - going high elf also gives us detect magic which we can cast for free x1/LR or as a ritual and provides a self-generated target for drain magic
 - blood.hunter martial/class features (fighting style, crimson rite, extra attack, brand of castigation, rite focus) do more for damage than base rogue sneak attack scaling 
 - blood.hunter d10 hit dice + shield of faith and defensive duelist makes operating as a melee rogue more tenable even with self damage from crimson rite/blood curse usage 
 - crossbow expert is the cornerstone feat: 
   (i) allows us to go into melee and dual wield hand-crossbow + dagger; wielding dagger in off-hand enables defensive duelist for big boost to AC in melee
   (ii) hand-crossbow benefits from archery fighting style (offsetting delayed ASI's), while crossbow expert essentially reproduces two weapon fighting (+DEX to BA attacks)
 - using a dagger (nick mastery) in our off-hand keeps our bonus action free for casting spells, activating crimson rite, blood curses, or cunning actions
 - starting @ lvl-07, we develop multiple attack patterns (see summary): ones that leverage nick to keep our BA free, and ones that use BA weapon attacks for more damage 
 - in addition to spell slots, blood hunter also gets us blood curse of marked, which can be amplified to auto-generate advantage for sneak attack; at later levels 
   profane soul also gets us shining smite (via revealed arcana) which we can use to boost damage and generate advantage for sneak attack; it also can work w/drain magic 
   and synergizes w/lvl-09 magic stealer feature (magical sabotage) which imposes disadvantage on the save against shining smite (maximizing efficiency of scarce spells) 
 - blood curse of the marked also works with hexblade rite focus, giving us additional rite damage while facilitating sneak attacks 
 - in tier-04, once we get good enough cantrip scaling and our INT is high enough, we can use profane soul mystic frenzy ability to do: true-strike + BA:attack to 
   deal more damage than even our attack combos that use extra-attack (assuming we don't need our BA, again see below). 
 - in general, we start building DEX and then shift to INT in late game to maximize spell damage/DC; going DEX first means we can use cunning strikes effectively for 
   control as needed once we get that ability (lvl-11). IMO this is better than going for an INT-first true-strike build which would handicap cunning strike until 
   very late in our career. 
 - overall, we provide out-of-combat utility through our excellent skills/ability checks, varied skill distribution and artisan's/thieves tools, and class/subclass 
   features (e.g., hunter's bane, grim psychometry in late game). In combat we are a single-target DPR monster. The main downside of this build will be our weak mental 
   saves (WIS, CHA). You could take mage slayer over defensive duelist if you really wanted, but I think you just lean in to it and make it a part of the character 
   identity (failure is fun).

BACKSTORY AND THEMATIC OVERVIEW: 

Wherever there is a city of consequence, there is an elven library. This is simply a fact of the world, as unremarkable as dwarven forges or halfling inns. The elves did not invent the written word, but they perfected its keeping — and over millennia, their archival traditions became the standard by which all serious collections are measured. From the grand athenaeum of a human capital to the modest reading room of a provincial trade town, the organizational principles are elven: the indexing systems, the preservation rites, the classification hierarchies, even the architecture of the stacks. Other races staff these institutions too — humans, gnomes, the occasional dwarf with a bookish streak — but the senior positions, the old knowledge, and the founding charters belong to the elves, and the cultural gravity of that heritage shapes everything. It is an influence so deeply woven into civic life that most people never think to question it, the way one does not question who built the roads.

In every such library there is a **Quillkeepers' Circle** — a modest clerical guild responsible for the unglamorous work that keeps the institution running: copying damaged texts, cataloguing new acquisitions, maintaining indices, shelving returns, managing correspondence between branches. Quillkeepers are a familiar sight across the civilized world, recognizable by their uniform grey robes and their signature tool: an articulated glove fitted with pen nibs, minor enchantments for page-turning and text-scanning, and slender finger-slots for holding different quills. Scholars barely notice them. Patrons look through them. They are, by design, the most forgettable people in the building.

The **Sisters of the Quill** hide among them. Not every Quillkeeper is a Sister — in fact almost all are exactly what they appear to be, dutiful archivists doing honest work — but scattered through the Circles of libraries across nations and continents are women who are something else entirely. They are orphan girls, selected young for the raw arcane potential in their blood, and subjected to the Hunter's Bane — an ancient and agonizing alchemical ritual that severs the subject's connection to the Weave and transmutes that inherent magical potential into enhanced physical prowess and augmented arcane senses. Their Quills are arcane-mechanical weapons disguised as clerical tools: fitted to the hand like a second skin, their eldritch engines fed not by conventional magic but by the wearer's own blood. Fine channels woven through the glove's interior press against the skin, drawing just enough to keep the mechanism primed — a constant, low ache that every Sister learns to ignore the way one ignores a heartbeat. In its mundane mode, this trickle of vitality powers the small conveniences any Quillkeeper enjoys: pages flipped at a gesture, search terms highlighted across an open codex, pen nibs held steady for transcription. But when the pen nibs are swapped for needle-thin bolts, the engine drinks deeper. The glove flushes warm, the channels bite, and the same life force that riffled harmlessly through parchment is concentrated into a killing stroke that drives steel through leather, chain, and bone. Every bolt a Sister fires costs her something. 

The Sisters are the killing edge of the **Aeloria Vaelithar** — the *Keepers of the Unwritten* — an ancient and morally ambiguous elven covenant that has warded the Material Plane against extraplanar threats since before human memory, and has never hesitated to lie, murder, or topple governments to do it. Their name refers to the histories they ensure never get written: the incursions they silenced, the rulers they removed, the planes they sealed shut — all erased, all kept only in the vaults beneath their libraries, in books that no one outside the order will ever read. The libraries are not merely a cover — they are the infrastructure. A network of reading rooms and archives spanning every kingdom gives the Vaelithar eyes and ears in every court, every university, every city of power, staffed by people so mundane that no one thinks twice about their presence.

Every Sister is bonded to her Quill from the moment she is old enough to wear it. The bonding is part of the Bane — the weapon becomes a vessel for the eldritch residue that the transformation couldn't fully burn away, and the girl's magic flows into the mechanism through her blood the way water finds a crack. The Quill learns her. It learns the rhythm of her pulse, the composition of her blood, the exact threshold of what she can give before she falters. In return, it gives her power — but it is always taking, always feeding, a symbiosis that never quite stops feeling like a wound. The Sisters speak of their Quills the way warriors speak of their swords: *this is mine, there are many like it, but this one is mine.* It is devotion, dependency, and damage all braided together — less a partnership than a scar the girl and her weapon share. She sleeps with the glove on. She feels its weight when it is absent like a phantom limb. Whether the mechanism hums in response to her touch because of engineering or because of something older is a question the Sisters do not ask.

Our character doesn't know that she descends from one of the most powerful elven bloodlines in history, a lineage tracing back to Corellon himself — only that the Bane took to her like fire to dry kindling. But the Bane couldn't take everything. In most Sisters, the transformation is total: the connection to the Weave is fully severed, the magic reforged into the body, and what remains flows only through the Quill. In her, something survived — a thread of raw potential so deep that even the Bane couldn't cauterize it. She began to notice it quietly, privately: the way she could feel a spell on someone's skin like a change in temperature, the way she could pull at an enchantment and watch it unravel into energy she could use. It wasn't part of her training. No other Sister could do it. She told no one — because a girl raised in the Sisterhood learns early that being different is dangerous, and being useful in the wrong way is worse. It is a secret she carries alongside all the others, one more thing to hide, one more question she cannot safely ask. Now, cut loose and alone, that question is one more thread to pull. Something has gone wrong. Her handler is dead or vanished, her network has gone dark, and she has washed up alone in the world for the first time, stripped of purpose and orders, carrying a body built for violence and a mind trained in secrets but utterly unprepared for the simple, terrifying business of living a free life.

KEY THEMATIC IDEAS AND DM HOOKS 

- **The Aeloria Vaelithar ("Keepers of the Unwritten")**: An ancient, Freemason-like elven secret society whose power is built on the backbone of the elven library network — a sprawling, continent-spanning infrastructure of archives, reading rooms, and athenaeia embedded in virtually every city of consequence. The libraries are real, functional, and respected civic institutions; the Vaelithar operate in the spaces between, using this unparalleled access to information and proximity to power as the foundation for intelligence-gathering, political manipulation, assassination, and extraplanar defense. Publicly dismissed as myth or conspiracy theory. Their name reflects their function: they are the custodians of the history that never happened, the crises no one remembers, the secrets locked in vaults beneath the stacks.

- **Elven library culture as pervasive institution**: Elves didn't invent libraries, but their archival traditions became the gold standard centuries ago, and now the organizational DNA of virtually every serious collection in the world is elven — the indexing systems, preservation rites, classification hierarchies, even the architecture. Other races work in these institutions, but elves dominate the senior ranks and the founding traditions. This cultural ubiquity is what makes the Vaelithar possible: their agents are everywhere because their libraries are everywhere, and no one questions the presence of quiet elven archivists in any city, court, or university. A DM can place this infrastructure in any setting and use it as a source of information, intrigue, or threat — every library the party enters is potentially a node in the network.

- **The Quillkeepers' Circles**: The mundane cover. A widespread, entirely legitimate guild of library clerks, copiers, indexers, and archivists present in every major library. Grey-robed, quiet, overlooked. Most Quillkeepers are ordinary people doing ordinary work — which is precisely what makes the Circles such effective camouflage. A DM can place Quillkeepers in any library the party visits, and the players will never know which ones are just scribes and which ones are Sisters — and neither will most of the Quillkeepers themselves.

- **The Sisterhood of the Quill**: A disturbing subsect hidden within the Quillkeepers. Orphan girls selected for magical potential, subjected to the Hunter's Bane ritual, and raised as hunter-assassins. Evokes child soldier programs, Black Widow's Red Room, and the Handmaids — themes of stolen agency, engineered identity, and bodies shaped by others' designs.

- **The Quill (Hexblade bond)**: Not a pen but a weapon disguised as one — an arcane-mechanical glove fitted to the hand, its eldritch engine powered by the wearer's own blood. Fine channels woven through the interior press against the skin, drawing vitality to fuel both mundane functions (page-flipping, text-scanning, transcription) and lethal ones (firing needle-bolts with killing force). The more violence a Sister commits, the more the Quill takes from her — every bolt costs a small piece of her life force. Each Sister is ritually bonded to her Quill in childhood, and the device learns her blood, her pulse, her limits. The bond mirrors the Hexblade pact: power flowing through an object that gives as much as it takes, that is at once an extension of the self and something disturbingly parasitic. She treats it with a tenderness she has never learned to extend to people. The Quills are a signature feature of the Sisterhood, and a DM could use them as a recognition signal — if the party ever encounters another woman wearing an unusually sophisticated archivist's glove, it means something. The blood-cost also provides narrative tension: a Sister who fights too much, too often, pays for it physically.

- **The Unburnt Thread (Magic Stealer origin)**: The Bane is supposed to sever a Sister's connection to the Weave entirely. In her case, it didn't. A residual capacity to touch, absorb, and recycle magical energy survived the transformation — but the Vaelithar don't know about it. She discovered it on her own, gradually, and kept it hidden out of self-preservation instinct. No other Sister she has met can do what she does, and she has no explanation for why she can. This is a secret she holds even from her own order — and as the ability grows stronger over the course of the campaign, it becomes harder to conceal and more urgent to understand. The truth, which she does not yet suspect, is that her bloodline was simply too potent for the Bane to fully extinguish.

- **Hidden lineage**: She unknowingly carries one of the most potent elven bloodlines in existence (direct descent from Corellon). This is why the Bane was so effective on her body — and why it failed to fully extinguish her magic. Neither she nor the Vaelithar understand the connection. If the truth surfaces, it could rewrite her relationship with the order entirely: did they recruit her by coincidence, or did someone arrange for a daughter of that bloodline to end up in an orphanage in the first place?

- **Cut loose**: At campaign start, her chain of command has collapsed — handler dead, network silent, possibly memories tampered with. She's adrift and, for the first time, making her own choices. The campaign is partly a mystery (what happened?) and partly an identity crisis (who is she without orders?).

- **Intelligence over charm**: CHA 8 means she can't smooth-talk or deceive; she hides her nature through cleverness, misdirection, and careful information control — playing dumb, changing the subject, knowing what not to say. A spy who survives on INT rather than charisma.

- **Sheltered naivety**: WIS 10 with low saves means she's perceptive and sharp-eyed (trained hunter) but emotionally vulnerable — easily frightened, manipulated, or charmed. Book-smart but life-ignorant; awkward in social situations not because she's shy but because she was raised in a cloister of assassin-librarians and has no frame of reference for normal life. Perhaps the Vaelithar purposefully keeps the Sister's sheltered and traumatized in order to make them more easily controlled. 

- **The quiet one**: Her cover identity as a Quillkeeper should feel real — she genuinely loves knowledge, languages, and the work of cataloguing and preserving texts. The violence underneath is something she compartmentalizes, not something she revels in.

- **Pop culture touchstones**: Black Widow (Red Room survivor, engineered killer finding her own identity), The Handmaid's Tale (systemic control of women's bodies/agency), John Wick (hidden world of assassins with codes and consequences), Alita: Battle Angel (amnesiac weapon discovering her own past), The Witcher (mutated hunters, tragic transformation, outsider status), Assassin's Creed (ancient order, hidden blades, Templar-style conspiracy), Full Metal Jacket (the ritualized, psychologically coercive bond between warrior and weapon).

NOTE: Starting as Blood Hunter rather than Rogue is a deliberate choice. Both classes share the same saving throw proficiencies (DEX, INT), so there's no difference there. The advantage comes from tool proficiencies: since multiclassing into Rogue at lvl-02 still grants one skill proficiency (bringing us to 4 class skills total — the same as if we'd started Rogue), starting Blood Hunter nets us Alchemist's Supplies proficiency for free.

Skills. Acrobatics is our physical proficiency (scales with DEX). Arcana and History flesh out our bookish backstory — Arcana plus Calligrapher's Supplies enables spell scroll crafting, and History will synergize with Grim Psychometry in when we get it in tier-04. Investigation and Perception are both thematic and come from our background. Survival from Keen Senses (elf lineage) is a key pickup, since it pairs with the advantage granted by Hunter's Bane.

Class Features. At this level we get Hunter's Bane (advantage on Survival/INT checks related to fey, fiends, or undead), martial weapon proficiency, and one blood curse. I take Blood Curse of the Marked — its base effect won't matter until lvl-03 (when we get Crimson Rite), but for now we can amplify it to grant ourselves advantage on one attack.

Equipment. Our starting gold (50gp Scribe + 150gp Blood Hunter) funds what is essentially our core kit for the entire career — same weapons, armor, and spellcasting setup from lvl-01 to lvl-20:
 - Hand crossbow + bolts — our primary weapon.
 - Dagger — offhand for two-weapon fighting.
 - Studded leather — AC 15 (AC 17 with Shield of Faith).
 - Thieves' tools — we gain proficiency next level, and these are harder to find in-world than other artisan's tools.
 - Ruby of the War Mage — attached to the hand crossbow (our "glove weapon"), letting us perform somatic and material components with our main hand. Not strictly essential (we could draw/stow each turn, and we'll eventually get War Caster), but it eliminates component management from the start, makes physical sense for a glove weapon, and avoids relying on finding one later.

Shopping List. Three priorities for when we have gold: (1) More daggers (at least 5 at 2gp each) so we can reliably use our two-weapon fighting pattern at range — if someone can lend us gold, even better. (2) Calligrapher's supplies (10gp) for scribing spell scrolls in downtime; the build is already designed around scarce spell slots, and scrolls further relax those constraints. Not urgent since we can't afford scroll crafting costs yet anyway. (3) Alchemist's supplies, which let us craft spy-useful items (acid, alchemist's fire) and identify magical substances.

Origin Feat: Magic Initiate (Cleric). The centerpiece here is Shield of Faith. We want a concentration spell that can last an entire combat, which we can later dispel via the Magic-Stealer Rogue's Drain Magic feature to recover spell slots. Shield of Faith fits perfectly — it's long-duration, and the +2 AC is genuinely needed while we're dual wielding in melee. Right now we can only cast it once per LR with no spell slots to recast, but that changes at lvl-05 with our Blood Hunter subclass. Thematically, I'd re-flavor it as "Arcane Shield" — something emanating from the Quill rather than a divine source. For cantrips, Guidance and Resistance both fit the "enhanced arcane senses" flavor of Hunter's Bane. We also get wizard cantrip from high elf and we take blade ward. Guidance is excellent out-of-combat utility (especially with our strong skill bonuses), and Resistance/Blade-Ward give us a decent defensive concentration options when Shield of Faith is spent. 

Combat Tactics. To maximize DPR, we Attack with the hand crossbow (main hand) and throw a dagger as a bonus action (offhand, two-weapon fighting). Ideally we stay at range to avoid disadvantage on crossbow attacks, but with only one dagger at creation we may need to close to melee. When we want to cast Shield of Faith or use Blood Curse of the Marked, the BA goes there instead of the offhand attack. Peak damage is 1d6+3+1d4 = 9 per round, though in practice it'll be lower given hit chance, limited daggers and competing BA uses. The annoying thing about this feature for a straight-classed Magic Stealer is its dependency on other creatures casting spells nearby. A huge defensive buff.

The key insight for Drain Magic is that we use it on ongoing spells that have outlived their usefulness. Shield of Faith is the perfect target: cast it in combat, and with a 10-minute duration it's usually still active when the fight ends. If another caster in the party has more impactful spells to recast, this flexibility is worth keeping in mind.

Pact Magic Upgrade. Our pact magic slots increase to lvl-02, we gain a new warlock spell preparation, and we get the Revealed Arcana subclass feature, which grants a patron-specific spell — Shining Smite for Hexblade. These changes interact significantly, so let's walk through the resulting spell reorganization and then discuss why it matters.

Spell Changes. Since Shining Smite is replacing Shield of Faith as our default combat concentration spell (explained below), we no longer need SoF on our spell list. Instead, we move Protection from Evil and Good over to our Magic Initiate (Cleric) slot, preserving both the free daily cast and the continuing-effect synergy with Drain Magic. PfEG remains valuable for its defensive utility against charm and frighten effects, which is especially important given our low WIS and CHA saves. This frees up PfEG's former warlock preparation slot, which we fill with Illusory Script — a flavor pick that is deeply on-brand for a spy and librarian. Magically encoded messages feel like a natural formalized communication method for the Vaelithar's upper echelons, and the spell pairs beautifully with our Calligrapher's Supplies proficiency. There is also a potential mechanical angle worth exploring: if your DM rules that creatures designated to read Illusory Script count as being "under the effect" of the spell for Drain Magic purposes, its 10-day duration and unlimited number of designations make it an incredibly versatile Drain Magic target. You could write something on a piece of paper, designate your whole party as readers, and then use Drain Magic on any of them throughout the day to recover spell slots. Thematically, imagine using our Calligrapher's Supplies to create something like a "scroll of spell recovery" — an object that looks and feels like a spell scroll but functions for slot recovery rather than spellcasting. Fun if your DM allows it, but no big deal if not — we can still proc Drain Magic via free casts of Detect Magic or PfEG.

For our new lvl-02 warlock spell preparation, we take Spider Climb. This gives us a meaningful mobility option in a build that doesn't have many. Our movement toolkit has been limited to Cunning Action dash/disengage, the Withdraw cunning strike, and a once-per-day Misty Step — all useful, but none of them address vertical movement or difficult terrain in the way Spider Climb does. For a spy and infiltrator, the ability to scale walls and traverse ceilings without a check opens up exploration and positioning possibilities that are hard to replicate otherwise. It also pairs well with our fighting style, since we can fire our hand crossbow perfectly well from a wall or ceiling at limited range while staying out of melee reach. Invisibility was the other strong contender here, and would certainly be a fine pick for a stealth-oriented character. But between our high Stealth floor (Reliable Talent), Cunning Action hide, and the general availability of Invisibility from other party members, I think Spider Climb fills a more unique gap in our capabilities.

Revealed Arcana — A Note on RAW. As written on DnD Beyond, Revealed Arcana lets you cast the signature spell using pact magic slots but only once per long rest. This is supposed to mirror the warlock's Mystic Arcanum, which grants free casts of spells above pact slot level. But we are getting Shining Smite, a lvl-02 spell, at the same time our pact slots become lvl-02. There is no reason we should not be able to cast it freely with our available slots, and any reasonable DM should agree. It is a slight departure from strict RAW, but worth noting.
We are also using the 2024 PHB version of Shining Smite rather than the 2014 Branding Smite specified on the profane soul wiki. The only difference is that the 2024 version adds advantage on attack rolls against the affected target, and this is what makes the spell worth building around.

Why Shining Smite Over Shield of Faith. This is the big shift at this level. From a pure personal-DPR perspective, Hex actually outdamages Shining Smite — three attacks over multiple rounds versus a one-time 2d6 burst. But casting lvl-01 spells with lvl-02 slots is bad value, and Shining Smite's real strength is not personal damage but rather the party-wide advantage it provides. Granting our entire party advantage against a key target for the duration of a fight is almost certainly worth more than the extra personal damage from Hex, and it also guarantees our own Sneak Attack procs every turn we hit.
The tradeoff is losing Shield of Faith's +2 AC, and there is no getting around that hit to survivability. But this is precisely why we went up to Rogue-08 before coming back for Shining Smite. With Evasion, Uncanny Dodge, Defensive Duelist, and our DEX now capped — plus whatever magic items we have accumulated by this point — we are durable enough to let go of SoF. We can still fall back on Blade Ward if we need a purely defensive option, and magic items that boost AC are far more common than ones that grant party-wide advantage on attacks.

Pact Slots at Lvl-02 — Other Benefits. Our Empower Sneak Attack now always procs at +2d6, since all of our self-cast spells are lvl-02 via pact slots. This is an increase from the previous mix of lvl-01 and lvl-02 procs. We can also now cast Misty Step with spell slots rather than relying solely on our once-per-LR free cast, which provides nice additional flexibility.

DPR. Switching from Hex to Shining Smite as our default concentration spell nets roughly +3d6 (10.5) damage per day from the more offensive spell, and the across-the-board lvl-02 Empower Sneak Attack adds another ~+1.1 to sustained DPR. We land in the 44–48 range for sustained DPR, still B-class for tier-03. Nova takes a small hit without Hex, down to about 59.5. We still have access to Hex if we want maximum burst, though we will be swapping it out next level. The slight dip in AC and nova is well worth the party-wide advantage and improved sustained damage.
