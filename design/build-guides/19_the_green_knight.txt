CONCEPT NOTES: This build makes use of several of the updated spells in the new 2024 PHB, specifically the updated shillelagh and true strike cantrips, as well as the level-4 `fount of moonlight' bard/druid spell. Here are a few notes on these spells. Shillelagh still takes a BA to cast and converts a club or quarterstaff into a weapon that uses your spellcasting modifier for attack and damage rolls. The difference is that now, casting the spell immediately converts the base damage of the weapon to 1d8, and this increases to 1d10 at level-5, a d12 at level-11, and 2d6 at level-17. You can also change the damage type of the shillelagh weapon to force damage (for overcoming resistances). True strike in 2024 is now an entirely different spell. Now it allows you to make an attack with a weapon (that you're proficient with) and use your spellcasting ability modifier for attack and damage rolls. Additionally, if the weapon hits, you can change its damage type to radiant. Additionally, starting at level-5, when you hit with true strike the attack does additional radiant damage. This extra radiant damage is 1d6 at level-5, 2d6 at level-11, and 3d6 at level-17. Finally, fount of moonlight is a concentration spell that makes each of your melee attacks do an additional 2d6 on hit. You can also use your reaction when you take damage to try and blind the creature that damaged you. Ok, so with that background, the concept for this build is to leverage the level-7 `war magic' ability of the eldritch knight, which allows you to replace one of your attacks with a cantrip when you take the attack action to both (i) make attacks with a shillelagh-ed weapon and (ii) use true strike. Once we reach level-7, dual wielding a shillelagh and another light weapon will allow us to make additional attacks with our bonus action. Ultimately, when we reach druid-7 (at level-16), we get fount of moonlight and then add 2d6 to each of our weapon attacks (we also get another 1d8 from elemental-fury::primal-strikes). For flavor, we should think of this character as a `primal-magic' eldritch knight. I didn't want to lean to heavily into the fey side of this, so think more highlander/druidic warrior. The green knight moniker is meant to capture this idea.

NOTE: Ok we're fighter-3 and we get our subclass. We're going eldritch knight. Right away we get war-bond, which we should use on our quarterstaff so that we can't be disarmed of it, and we can summon it if we don't have it for some reason. This is nice since we won't be very effective attacking with anything that doesn't have shillelagh cast on it. We also get spellcasting. At level-3 an eldritch knight knows two wizard cantrips and can prepare 3 spells, two of which must be from the abjuration or evocation school. For cantrips, we're going to take true-strike, which is new and (vastly) improved in 2024. We won't actually be using it for another couple levels, but it is an essential pick for this build (see concept note above). Blade ward has also been reworked in 2024. Now you cast it as a reaction in response to a creature targeting you with a melee attack and it imposes disadvantage on the attack. 

For 1st-level spells, I like absorb elements (if it's included in the 2024 spell list) since our DEX save isn't the greatest, so we're vulnerable to AOE spells/abilities, many of which do elemental damage. The spell also gives us a way to do a little extra damage if we tank an elemental hit, and it's thematic for our primal eldritch knight. Of course shield is another good option, but right now our AC is pretty high, plus we get more uses of second wind than we did in 2014, so we have better healing. Taking blade ward as our second wizard cantrip also partially makes up for not taking shield. Next up is earth tremor (evocation). This spell requires all creatures within 10ft of you to make a DEX save or take 1d6 bludgeoning damage and fall prone. It also makes the area within the 10ft radius difficult terrain. Other evocation options, such as burning hands, are better for straight damage, but knocking enemies prone is really good for us since it reduces their movement and gives us advantage on subsequent attacks. We can also combine earth tremor with action surge to knock prone and then attack. Right now we can also knock prone with our quarterstaff (topple), but this will change in a few levels. 

There's also an important aside that comes up for us here. In the new 2024 PHB, the rules on casting multiple spells in a turn have been changed. The new rules state that you can only *expend 1 spell slot* on your turn. This means that we can cast spells granted to us by features/abilities and still cast a different spell using a spell slot on our turn. For us, this applies to the free casting of ensnaring strike granted to us by our magic-initiate feat. For example, we could (i) cast ensnaring strike without expending a spell slot as a BA using magic initiate, then cast earth tremor using a spell slot, then action-surge and attack, procing our ensnaring strike. This combo would leave an enemy (i) prone, (ii) restrained, and (iii) in difficult terrain. This type of use of ensnaring strike will also apply with other spell combos we get at future levels. But it's worth mentioning it here. 

Finally, I think expeditious retreat is a necessary inclusion. We are at our best as a melee attacker, so mobility skills are essential to help us get on top of enemies. This spell gives us a BA dash as long as we're concentrating on it. Use it to chase down escaping/squishy enemies then knock them prone/restrain them. Note that all of our eldritch knight spells require somatic components (except true strike, which we aren't using right now) so we can't have our shield in hand when we cast them. The only one this is really a problem for is absorb elements. Depending on the situation, consider dropping the shield so you can cast effectively. This will only matter for this level (level-3).

NOTE: Big level here. First, at fighter-7 we get the iconic eldritch knight war magic feature. In 2014, this feature allowed the EK to cast a cantrip and then make a weapon attack as a BA. Now in 2024, it allows us to replace one of the attacks we make when we take the attack action with a cantrip, freeing up our BA. As alluded to in the concept note above, this ability forms the core of our shillelagh + true-strike build. With war magic in hand, we drop our shield and quarterstaff, switch our fighting style over to two-weapon fighting (TWF), and start dual wielding a club (I'd reflavor this as a tonfa or night-stick) and a scimitar. As always, we're constantly casting shillelagh on the club while we're walking around and draw our scimitar when a fight starts. Then, when we take the attack action, we replace our first attack with a casting of the true-strike cantrip using our scimitar. This does 2d6+INT on hit. Since scimitars have the `nick' property, if we hit then we can make an additional attack with our shillelagh-tonfa as part of the same attack. Since we switched our fighting style to TWF, this does 1d10+INT on hit and slows the enemy 10ft. Then we take our second attack with our shillelagh-tonfa to do another 1d10+INT damage. So 2d10+2d6+3INT or 33 damage with our basic rounds of attacks, which is very good. Notably, we can do all of this and still free up our bonus action for things like second wind/tactical shift, expeditious retreat dash, or ensnaring strike. Our highest damage combo in a single round would be: 
 
  BA:ensnaring-strike(lvl-2) + attack(1):true-strike:scimitar(nick) + attack(2):shillelagh(slow) + attack(3):shillelagh(slow)
  + action-surge + attack(4):true-strike:scimitar(nick) + attack(5):shillelagh(slow) + attack(6):shillelagh(slow)

Which would do a whopping: 2d6 + 2*(2d10+2d6+3INT) = 73 damage. That's a very high single-round burst for level-7! 

At EK-7 we also get access to level-2 wizard spells. There are a lot of great options here. I went with mirror image since our AC takes a hit (to 16) now that we're dual-wielding instead of carrying a shield. Mirror image is non-concentration and doesn't require material components, so we can cast it to boost survivability when we need it. It's also been buffed in the 2024 PHB (now your images are protected by your AC, instead of being vulnerable to every attack). Next, I went with blindness/deafness. I took this over other options for a few reasons (i): enemies who are blinded have disadvantage on attacks and attackers have advantage against them; this simultaneously boosts our survivability against blinded opponents and means we have advantage on the many attacks we make each round, (ii) it's not concentration and doesn't require material components, so we can cast it on a key enemy and still use ensnaring strike/expeditious retreat, (iii) it's not commonly resisted; less than 5% of monsters have resistance/immunity to blindness, so it's a general purpose debuff that's likely to work in most situations (in contrast to something like hold-person or ray of enfeeblement), (iv) we have ways of restraining and slowing enemies, so spells like rime's binding ice or earthen grasp feel a bit redundant. We can also use it with our ensnaring-strike action surge combo in order to blind and restrain an enemy in one turn. Finally, even though it's a CON save, our save DC is high (DC-16), so it still has a good chance of working/sticking. Of course, if you disagree and think blindness/deafness is garbage feel free to pick your favorites. Other honorable mention level-2 spells include: hold person, ray of enfeeblement, suggestion, web, rime's binding ice, earthen grasp, misty step, invisibility. 

In terms of general playstyle, I think this character is now built to have great sustained DPS (TWF, shillelagh, true-strike) with some good defensive options (blade ward, absorb elements, mirror image, second wind) to stay in the fight, and the ability to selectively deploy some powerful control options (ensnaring strike, earth tremor, blindness/deafness). All this while still having great out of combat proficiencies (insight, survival, nature, stealth, arcana) to play around with. Sounds like a super fun character to play!

Moving on from wild-shape, the big draw at druid-7 is that we get access to 4th level druid spells. Freedom of movement means we are unrestricted by difficult terrain (including, for example the terrain created by our plant growth spell), we can't be paralyzed or restrained or have our speed reduced in any way by magical effects. We can also use 5ft of movement to automatically escape a grapple or non-magical restraint and we have no penalties for moving underwater. I think this spell is a really nice addition to our arsenal of things that helps us fight mages and magic users, who might often try to inhibit our restrain us with magical abilities. Plus, if we wanted we could cast this for free once per day using our natural recovery feature. It's also a 1h buff and doesn't require concentration so we will have no issues using it in combination with our other main spells. In terms of prepared spells, there are some good ones we now have access to. Stoneskin -- which gives you resistance to non-magical bludgeoning, piercing, and slashing damage -- got a rework in the 2024 PHB and is no longer concentration (check this!). This could be a nice boost to our survivability. Polymorph is a fantastic spell with a lot of out-of-combat utility. Locate creature can be situationally clutch. Grasping vine also got a rework in 2024 (it now does damage and grapples when it hits), but would still use our WIS modifier to hit, so not great. Control water is also great, but also uses our WIS mod. 

But in truth, there are two main reasons we're excited about 4th level spells. The first is fire shield. Fire shield is an amazing spell for low-WIS melee druids. It's not concentration (though does require material components), lasts 10m, and provides us with a shield that either gives us resistance to cold (warm shield) or fire (chill shield) damage. Fire damage is super common, and we don't have very high AC, so I would probably default to the chill shield. Our chill shield also does damage (2d8 cold) to any creature that hits us with a melee attack. This will be very good for us since we will be in melee a lot and have low AC, allowing us to proc it reliably. As an aside, being able to cast the chill shield also kind of completes our primal elementalist identity, in a way. We have fire options with produce flame (and fire shield), lightning options with shocking grasp and lightning bolt, earth/plant options with ensnaring strike, earth tremor, and plant growth, and now a cold-based option with the chill shield. Of course, we also have primal-strikes and elementalism for all-elemental flavor. But for whatever reason this kind of elemental balance satisfies me, and is another reason for taking the fire-shield spell. We have to drop pass without trace to get it, but this seems like a fair tradeoff. And obviously, we can still take PWT in situations where we know we're going to need it. 

Saving the best for last, the PRIMARY reason we're stoked about 4th level spells is, of course, the new spell `fount of moonlight' (queue choir sound). So what does this spell do? While we're concentrating on it (up to 10m) fount of moonlight (FoM) (i) wreathes our body in cool light for the duration, causing us to emit bright light in a 20ft radius and dim light for an additional 20ft, (ii) gives us resistance to radiant damage, (iii) allows our melee attacks to do an additional 2d6 radiant damage on hit, and (iv) gives us a special use of our reaction where we can try to blind a target in response to taking damage from them. Ok so we love pretty much every part of this freaking awesome spell. First off, if for some reason we are fighting in the dark and don't have darkvision up, fount of moonlight gives us a light source. Second, while radiant damage isn't super common, any damage resistance is great for our low AC character, and we can stack it with fire shield to get resistance to fire and radiant damage. Third, we already have been using the true-strike cantrip to deal radiant damage with weapon attacks and the blindness/deafness spell to blind enemies for a long while now. So the extra damage and new reaction ability are very thematically consistent with our character's previous abilities. It does require concentration, so we can't use it and cast our other faves like ensnaring strike, hold person, or heat metal. But it doesn't require material components -- so no awkward drawing/stowing of weapons to cast it -- and we can still use blindness/deafness, silence, plant growth, earth tremor, lightning bolt and all our combat utility/mobility spells (jump, shield, absorb elements, misty step, mirror image, dispel magic, fire shield) while we're concentrating on it.  

And finally and most importantly, we do 3 weapon attacks per round by default, and can do up to 6 if we action surge. So this fount of moonlight (FoM) represents a HUUGE boost to our damage! For our base round of attacks, we would now do 2d12 (shillelagh) + 3d6 (scimitar true-strike) + 1d8 (primal-strike) + 6d6 (FoM) + 15 = 64 damage per round. And our best nova move would be to do six attacks with action-surge while this spell is active. This would do 123.5 per round, on average. While this isn't going to be top of the list for either sustained DPR or nova damage, it is going to be high up on both lists. We're also going to get another boost to our damage when our cantrips improve next level. So, woo-hoo, moving on!

NOTE: I don't usually map out builds beyond level 17, but I like what we've built here enough to see it through all the way. At level-18, we turn back to fighter for fighter-10. Staying with druid would get us some nice things, including 5th level druid spells and, eventually, a 7th level spell slot. But I think our character's core identity has always been more fighter. And now that we are all leveled up in druid, going back to fighter gives us some very powerful abilities. First off, at fighter-10, we get another use of second wind/tactical mind (so up to 4 per day). Very nice ability for both in and out of combat, so it's nice to have more uses of it. We also get another weapon mastery, which doesn't really matter at all. I chose rapiers, but pick whatever you want and go true-strike with it, It won't be as good as our current nick-based dual-wielding setup. At level-10, eldritch-knights also get another wizard cantrip and gain the eldritch strike ability. The eldritch strike ability says that when we hit a creature with an attack using a weapon, that creature has disadvantage on the next saving throw it makes against a spell you cast before the end of your next turn. The mind sliver cantrip says that creatures that fail an INT save take damage and must also subtract 1d4 from the next save they make before the end of our next turn. So you can see where we're going with this. With our war magic ability to cast cantrips as part of the attack action, we can stack these abilities. 

Specifically, we can use our (i) BA to (up)cast ensnaring strike, then take the attack action on our turn and (ii) cast mind-sliver with our first attack then (iii) hit them with our shillelagh with our second attack, giving them disadvantage via eldritch strike and procing ensnaring strike. This would mean that the target creature would have a -7 to their STR save, on average, against our DC-19 ensnaring strike. So with this combo we could very reliably restrain creatures that are Large or smaller, even if they have substantial bonuses to their STR save. Depending on how you interpret the eldritch strike ability, if you continued making weapon attacks on the enemy on your next turn, then you could continually impose disadvantage on the save to break out of the ensnaring vines. It depends on whether or not you interpret the ability as applying to subsequent saves made against spells that you cast on turns prior to the attack that applies eldritch strike. I could see DM's ruling either way on this. If your DM does say eldritch strike does disadvantage subsequent saves, then you can also make use of this ability with action surge. Specifically, you could cast a spell like blindness/deafness or hold-person, then if it works action surge and do mind-sliver + eldritch strike to give them a major debuff to their next save against the spell. But even if your DM rules the other way -- i.e., that you can't permanently impose disadvantage on saves to escape a spell by continuing to attack an enemy -- the mind-sliver + eldritch-strike + ensnaring-strike combo is still really good. And, of course, there's also the `standard' 2-turn combo where you just mind-sliver + eldritch-strike them on one turn, and then cast a spell on your next. So anyway you slice it, this is a great cantrip + ability combo to get.

At fighter-10 we also get to prepare another wizard spell (so up to 7). I chose vortex warp (from strixhaven) since it's non-concentration, requires no material components, can be used on allies and enemies alike, and upcasts reasonably well. Even though we have invested some of our spellcraft into improving our mobility, we still aren't going to be flying around the battlefield, so to speak. Vortex warp gives us the ability to (attempt to) make our enemies come to us. Obviously, we also discussed other good options at previous levels. They include: binding ice, suggestion, mind whip, earth bind, borrowed knowledge, enlarge/reduce, invisibility, ray of enfeeblement, and so on. Pick your favorites. And that's it for level-18.
